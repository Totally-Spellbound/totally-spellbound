## Part A: Deep Data Review — Understanding Your Business

You've been collecting data for three months now — every online order, every in-shop sale through the POS, every discount code used. That data is gold, and Shopify Sidekick is going to help you dig through it without any spreadsheets or complicated reports.

Think of this like taking stock of the shop, but instead of counting what's on the shelves, you're finding out what your customers actually want.

### Step 1: The Big Questions to Ask Sidekick

Open your Shopify admin (the screen your sons set up for you), and tap the Sidekick chat button. Then ask these questions, one at a time. Write down or screenshot each answer — you'll need them in a moment.

- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"What are my top 10 best selling products overall?"**
- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"What are my top 5 best selling products online?"**
- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"What are my top 5 best selling products in-store?"**
- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"Are there products that sell well online but not in-store, or vice versa?"**
- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"What day of the week do I get the most online orders?"**
- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"What was my total online revenue last month?"**
- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"What was my total in-store revenue last month?"**
- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"Which discount codes have been used the most?"**
- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"How many repeat customers do I have?"**

Don't worry if some of the answers surprise you. That's the whole point — the data often tells you things you wouldn't have guessed.

### Step 2: What to Do With the Answers

Now you've got your answers, here's what to do with each one. Go through these one by one:

**Products that sell well online:**
- [ ] Make sure these are always in stock — don't let them run out
- [ ] If something sells really quickly every time you restock it, consider raising the price by a pound or two. If people want it, they'll still buy it, and you'll earn a bit more on each sale
- [ ] Make sure the photos for these products are your very best ones — they're your shop window

**Products that sell well in-store but not online:**
- [ ] Have a look at their photos — are they as good as they could be? Sometimes a crystal looks gorgeous in person but the photo doesn't do it justice
- [ ] Read the product description — does it capture what makes this item special? If not, ask Sidekick to help you rewrite it
- [ ] Consider featuring these products in your next Facebook live — sometimes people just need to see them "in real life" to want them

**Products that don't sell anywhere:**
- [ ] Put them on sale — a clearance section on your website can shift things that are just sitting there
- [ ] Feature them in a Facebook live with a special deal — sometimes all a product needs is someone talking about it with enthusiasm
- [ ] If they still don't sell after being on sale for a month, it might be time to stop ordering them. That's not failure — that's smart business

**If one day of the week gets more online orders:**
- [ ] This is useful! It might line up with when Xyla posts certain content, or the day after a Facebook live
- [ ] If you know your busy day, make sure you're checking for orders that morning so you can get them posted quickly
- [ ] You might even ask Xyla to schedule bigger posts on the day before your busy day to ride that wave

**If you have repeat customers:**
- [ ] These people are your VIPs — they love what you do
- [ ] Write down their names. You'll be using this list later in Part G when we set up the loyalty programme
- [ ] These are the people who would probably leave you a lovely review if you asked nicely — and reviews help other people trust your shop

## Part B: Increasing Facebook Live Frequency

Your Facebook lives with Kirsty (and your daughter-in-law) have been brilliant for getting people to the website. Every live with its own discount code means you can actually see how many sales each live generates. Now let's do more of them.

### Step 1: Review Your Live Performance

- [ ] Ask Sidekick: **"How many times has each of my discount codes been used?"**
- [ ] Look at which lives brought in the most sales — was it the ones where Kirsty showed crystals? The ones with gift ideas? The ones where you showed the engraver?
- [ ] Write down what worked best — you'll use this to plan themed lives

### Step 2: Talk to Your Team About Going Weekly

- [ ] Have a chat with Kirsty and your daughter-in-law about doing lives every week instead of every two weeks
- [ ] If weekly feels like too much for one person, suggest alternating: Kirsty does one week, your daughter-in-law does the next
- [ ] Pick a regular day and time — consistency helps people know when to tune in (for example, every Thursday at 7pm)
- [ ] If weekly really isn't possible right now, even going from fortnightly to three times a month is progress

### Step 3: Create a Unique Discount Code for Every Live

- [ ] Before each live, tell Sidekick: **"Create a discount code called [LIVETHEME] for 10% off, valid for 48 hours"**
- [ ] For example: "CRYSTALHEALING10" or "NEWARRIVALS10" or "GIFTLIVE10"
- [ ] The person doing the live mentions the code at least twice — near the start and near the end
- [ ] The 48-hour time limit creates a bit of urgency — people are more likely to use it if they know it expires

### Step 4: Plan Themed Lives Based on Your Data

Here are some theme ideas. Pick the ones that match what your data says sells well:

- [ ] **"Crystal Healing Live"** — Group crystals by what they're used for: calm and relaxation, protection, love and relationships, confidence. People love learning which crystal is right for them
- [ ] **"New Arrivals Live"** — Every time you get a new delivery from your supplier, do a live unboxing and showing everything that's just arrived. People love seeing things first
- [ ] **"Gift Ideas Under £10 Live"** — This is brilliant for attracting new customers who might not want to spend a lot on their first order. It gets them through the door
- [ ] **"Custom Engraving Live"** — Show the laser engraver in action! Let people watch you engrave something live and take orders during the stream. More on this below...

### Step 5: The Live Engraving Idea (This Could Be Your Secret Weapon)

This one deserves its own section because it could be really special. People absolutely LOVE watching things being made. There's something mesmerising about watching a laser engrave a name onto a crystal or a spell jar.

- [ ] Consider doing a live where the engraver is the star — show it working, explain what it can do, and take custom orders right there during the live
- [ ] People watching can comment "I'd like my name on an amethyst!" and you (or whoever is presenting) can write down the orders
- [ ] This creates excitement because they're watching their item being made in real time

**A note for you, Alison:** If you ever feel comfortable going on camera yourself for one of these engraving lives, it could be absolutely wonderful. You know your products better than anyone, and your passion for them shines through when you talk about them. But there is absolutely no pressure — only do this if and when it feels right for you. Kirsty or your daughter-in-law can do the engraving lives just as well.

## Part C: Maximising Craft Fairs

You've already been doing craft fairs, and now you've got business cards pointing people to the website. Brilliant. But let's make every single craft fair work harder for you. Every person who visits your table — even if they don't buy anything that day — should leave knowing about your website.

### Step 1: Before Each Fair

- [ ] **Update the craft fair schedule page on your website.** Tell Sidekick: **"Add [fair name] on [date] at [location] to my craft fair schedule page"**
- [ ] **Create a discount code just for this fair.** Tell Sidekick: **"Create a discount code called SPRINGFAIR10 for 10% off, with no expiry date"** (use the name of the fair so you can track it later — for example, CHURCHFAIR10, CARNIVAL10, SCHOOLFAIR10)
- [ ] **Print a small sign for your table.** Something simple like:

  > **Shop online at totallyspellbound.com**
  > Use code **SPRINGFAIR10** for 10% off your first online order!

- [ ] Make sure you've got plenty of business cards packed and ready
- [ ] Charge your card reader if you use one, and make sure your POS is ready to go

### Step 2: At the Fair

- [ ] **Hand out business cards to absolutely everyone** — even people who just have a quick browse and walk away. Especially those people! They might go home and order something later
- [ ] **Mention the website and discount code to every single customer.** It feels a bit repetitive, but most people will only hear it once. Something like: "If you see anything you like but want to think about it, it's all on our website — and there's a discount code on the card!"
- [ ] If someone picks something up, looks at it, and puts it back, that's your cue: **"You can find that on our website too — and the card has a discount code for your first order"**
- [ ] If someone says **"I'll think about it"** — smile, hand them a card, and say: **"No problem at all! Everything's on our website, and there's 10% off your first online order with the code on the card"**

### Step 3: After the Fair

- [ ] **Check how the discount code performed.** Ask Sidekick: **"How many times has the discount code SPRINGFAIR10 been used?"**
- [ ] **Write down which fairs brought in online sales afterwards.** Over time, you'll build a picture of which fairs are worth going back to and which ones aren't
- [ ] **Update your craft fair schedule page.** Tell Sidekick: **"Remove [past fair name] from my craft fair schedule page"** — keep the page looking current and up to date
- [ ] If a fair brought in zero online orders, think about whether it's worth doing again — or whether you need to hand out more cards and mention the website more

### Step 4: Working Towards 4 Fairs Per Month

More fairs means more people seeing your products and getting your business card. Here's how to find more:

- [ ] **Search online for local craft fair listings** — try searching "craft fairs near [your area]" or "craft fairs [your county] 2026"
- [ ] **Join Facebook groups for craft fair organisers in your area.** Search Facebook for "craft fairs [your area]" or "market stall traders [your county]" — these groups post available pitches all the time
- [ ] **Think about different types of events:**
  - Church hall fairs
  - School fairs and fetes
  - Seasonal markets (Halloween is going to be massive for you!)
  - Summer carnivals and village fetes
  - Charity events
  - Christmas markets (book these early — they fill up fast)
- [ ] **Prioritise carnivals** — your data shows these are your best earners, bringing in up to £250 in a day. If you can get into more carnivals, do it
- [ ] Build towards doing one fair per week. If some weeks you do two, even better. But don't burn yourself out — find a pace that works for you

## Part D: Expanding Personalised Products

Your laser engraver and 3D printer give you something most small shops don't have: the ability to make custom, one-of-a-kind items. That's a real competitive advantage. People pay more for something made just for them, and they can't get it from Amazon.

### Step 1: Check What's Already Selling

- [ ] If they're selling — wonderful! Time to add more options
- [ ] If they're not selling yet, have a look at the listings: are the photos clear? Do the descriptions explain exactly what someone gets? Can people easily see that the item will be personalised for them?

### Step 2: Create More Personalised Listings

Here are some ideas for new personalised products. You don't have to do all of them — pick the ones that feel right for your shop and your equipment:

**Idea 1: Custom 3D Printed Crystal Stand**

- [ ] Photograph each one with a crystal sitting on it

- [ ] In the listing, show all the style and colour options so customers know what they can pick

**Idea 2: Engraved Spell Jar**
- [ ] This one is very popular in the witchy community — spell jars with someone's name, a date, or a short intention engraved on them
- [ ] Engrave a sample jar and photograph it
- [ ] Clean up the photo in Photoroom
- [ ] Add it to Shopify. Tell Sidekick: **"Help me write a product description for a personalised engraved spell jar. Customers can choose what text to engrave"**

**Idea 3: Personalised Witchy Gift Box**

**Idea 4: Custom Engraved Candle Holder**
- [ ] If your laser engraver can handle candle holders (test on one first!), this could be lovely
- [ ] Engrave a sample with a name or short message
- [ ] Photograph it, clean up in Photoroom, and add to Shopify with Sidekick's help

### Step 3: Set Clear Expectations on Every Personalised Listing

This is important — custom items take time to make, and customers need to know that upfront so nobody gets frustrated.

- [ ] Add this (or something similar) to every personalised product listing: **"Made to order just for you. Posted within 3-5 working days"**
- [ ] If it's a busy period (like the run-up to Christmas), you might need to extend that to 5-7 days — update the listings when needed

### Step 4: Pricing Personalised Items Properly

Please don't undercharge for custom work, Alison! Your time and skill are worth paying for. Here's how to work out a fair price:

- [ ] **Add up the costs:**
  - What the materials cost you (jar, crystal, stand, etc.)
  - Your time — even if it only takes 15 minutes, that time has value
  - Postage and packaging
  - A profit margin on top — this is your business, not a charity!

- [ ] **Check what others charge.** Have a look on Etsy or other websites for similar personalised witchy items. You don't need to be the cheapest — you need to be fair. People expect to pay more for something custom-made
- [ ] **A good rule of thumb:** Custom items should cost at least 30-50% more than a similar non-personalised item. If a plain spell jar is £8, an engraved one should be at least £12-15
- [ ] Don't be afraid of your prices. If someone is asking you to personalise something, they've already decided they want something special — they expect to pay for it

## Part E: Switching to Calculated Shipping (Optional)

Right now, you've probably got a flat shipping rate — something like £3.50 for everything. That works fine for most items, but if you're selling heavier things (like big crystals or gift boxes), you might be paying more in postage than you're charging the customer. That means you're losing money on shipping.

This section is completely optional. Only do it if you've noticed that postage is eating into your profits on certain items.

### Step-by-Step (Your Sons Will Do This Bit)

- [ ] Ask your sons to log into your Shopify admin
- [ ] They need to go to the **Shopify App Store** (it's like an app store for your shop — they'll know where it is)
- [ ] Search for the **Royal Mail shipping app** and install it
- [ ] Because you already added weights to every single product back in Section 1 (well done for doing that!), the app can work out the real Royal Mail postage cost automatically
- [ ] Once it's set up, customers will see the actual Royal Mail price at checkout instead of a flat rate
- [ ] **You can keep your "free shipping over £30" rule** alongside calculated rates — so if someone spends over £30, they still get free delivery. The calculated rates only apply to smaller orders
- [ ] After it's set up, do a test order yourself to make sure the prices look right

**Remember:** This is only worth doing if flat rate shipping is causing you to lose money. If flat rate is working fine and your margins are healthy, leave it as it is. Simple is good!

## Part F: Setting Up Email Marketing

You've got customers who have opted in to hear from you (they ticked the box at checkout). A simple monthly email is a lovely way to stay in touch, show them what's new, and bring them back to your website. Think of it like sending a postcard to a friend — "Here's what's new in the shop!"

### Step-by-Step Setup (Your Sons Will Help With This)

- [ ] **Ask your sons to set up a simple email template in Shopify Email.** This is built into Shopify — no extra apps needed, and it's free for up to 10,000 emails per month (you won't be sending anywhere near that many!)
- [ ] **The template should be simple and clean.** Something like:
  - A header that says "What's New at Totally Spellbound"
  - Space for 3-5 product photos with short descriptions
  - A "Shop Now" button that links to your website
  - Your shop's logo at the top
- [ ] Once the template is saved, your sons can show you how to use it — or you can ask Sidekick for help each month

### Step-by-Step: Sending Your Monthly Email

- [ ] **Pick a day each month to send it** — the first of the month is easy to remember, or you could send it after you get new stock in
- [ ] **Choose 3-5 products to feature.** These should be:
  - New arrivals that just came in
  - Your best sellers (people love knowing what's popular)
  - Any personalised products you want to highlight
  - Seasonal items (for example, protection crystals around Halloween)
- [ ] **Ask Sidekick for help writing it.** Tell Sidekick: **"Help me write a short monthly email for my customers featuring these 5 products: [list them]. Keep it friendly and short"**
- [ ] **Add the product photos** to the email using the template your sons set up
- [ ] **Send it!** Shopify Email will only send it to customers who opted in to marketing emails — that's the law (UK GDPR), and Shopify handles it for you automatically

### Important Rules for Email

- [ ] **Once a month is plenty.** Resist the temptation to send more. If you email people every week, they'll unsubscribe. Once a month keeps you in their mind without being annoying
- [ ] **Only email people who opted in.** Shopify handles this — you don't need to worry about it, but it's good to know why
- [ ] **Keep it short and visual.** People scan emails quickly. Big, beautiful photos and short descriptions work much better than long paragraphs
- [ ] **Always include a link to your website.** Every product photo should link to that product's page so people can buy with one click

You already have a physical loyalty card system in the shop — buy a certain number of items and get a discount. Let's bring that idea online. This doesn't need to be complicated. It's really just about noticing your best customers and saying thank you.

### Step 1: Find Your Repeat Customers

- [ ] Write down their names (or save Sidekick's response as a screenshot)

### Step 2: Create a Loyalty Discount Code

- [ ] Tell Sidekick: **"Create a 10% discount code called LOYAL10 that can only be used once per customer"**
- [ ] This means each person can only use it once, so you're giving a one-time thank you, not an unlimited discount

### Step 3: Send a Personal Thank You

- [ ] For each repeat customer, send them a short, personal email. You can do this through Shopify or just from your regular email. Something like:

  > *"Hi [name], I just wanted to say a huge thank you for being such a loyal customer of Totally Spellbound. It really means the world to me. As a little thank you, here's 10% off your next order — just use the code LOYAL10 at checkout. Thanks again, Alison x"*

- [ ] That's it! Keep it warm and personal. This is the online version of recognising a regular in the shop and giving them a little something extra
- [ ] People remember being appreciated. This kind of personal touch is something the big shops simply cannot do — but you can

### Step 4: Keep Checking for New VIPs

- [ ] Every month, ask Sidekick the same question: **"Which customers have ordered more than 4 times?"**
- [ ] If there are new names on the list, send them the same personal thank you with the loyalty code
- [ ] Over time, you'll build a lovely little group of regulars who feel connected to your shop

## Part I: Key Points to Remember

**The data tells you what to do next — trust it, not guesswork.** You've spent three months collecting real information about what your customers actually buy. Use it. If something sells well, do more of it. If something doesn't sell, stop guessing why and make a change.

**Personalised products are your competitive advantage — lean into them.** Your laser engraver and 3D printer mean you can offer things that nobody else in your village (or on most websites) can match. Custom, one-of-a-kind items made with care. That's special.

**Every craft fair is now a customer acquisition event, not just a one-day sale.** Yes, the money you make at the fair matters. But the business cards you hand out and the discount codes you share could bring in orders for weeks afterwards. A craft fair that makes £50 on the day but brings in £100 of online orders over the next month is actually a £150 fair.

**Craft fair discount codes tell you which fairs are worth repeating.** This is why every fair gets its own code — so you can look back and see which fairs actually brought people to your website. Do more of the fairs that work, and drop the ones that don't.

**Don't spam customers with emails — once a month is perfect.** A monthly email is a gentle reminder that you exist. More than that, and people start hitting the unsubscribe button. Quality over quantity, always.

**If something isn't working, change it — you have the data to know.** A product that doesn't sell, a craft fair that doesn't bring online orders, a live format that doesn't generate discount code use — these aren't failures. They're information. Change something and try again.

**You're building something here — momentum takes time but compounds.** Each month, you'll have more customers, more data, more reviews, more followers, more experience. It might feel slow sometimes, but look back at where you were three months ago. You didn't even have a website then! Every single thing you do now builds on what came before.
