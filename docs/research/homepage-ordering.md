# Homepage section ordering

Research checked 14 July 2026. This note separates observed evidence from the recommendation inferred for Totally Spellbound. It does not claim that a published study has tested this exact nine-section sequence.

## Recommendation

1. Hero
2. Trust bar
3. Browse the Collections
4. Consult the Oracle
5. New Manifestations
6. Ritual countdown, as an active campaign slot only; hide it otherwise
7. Shop editorial / visit-us story
8. Shop by Intention
9. Written in the Stars

This keeps the evidence-backed opening, then brings Totally Spellbound's most distinctive guided route forward. It also treats the countdown as a campaign slot rather than permanent page furniture. The order alone is insufficient: shorten the hero, product grid and late discovery grids or the Oracle will still arrive too late on mobile.

| Section | What it asks the visitor to think, feel or do | Why it belongs here |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hero | “I understand this shop and want to look further.” | It sets identity and gives the first shopping route. It should introduce, rather than consume, the mobile opening viewport. |
| Trust bar | “This is a real independent shop, with familiar payment and a person I can ask.” | Practical reassurance is most useful before the first browsing decision. This placement is an inference from homepage trust research; there is no cited causal test of a trust bar in position two. |
| Browse the Collections | “I can see what this shop sells and choose a clear department.” | Large-scale usability evidence is strongest here. First-time and mobile visitors use homepage category breadth to form a mental map. |
| Consult the Oracle | “Help me choose.” | This is both guided product finding and the strongest brand-specific interaction. It follows the ordinary catalogue map, so it does not obscure the range. The higher placement is an inference and depends on every answer producing useful, in-stock results. |
| New Manifestations | “These are real things I could buy now.” | Concrete products follow the two main orientation routes. Keep the edit short and genuinely new; the live eight-product set includes a sold-out item and Christmas products in July. |
| Ritual countdown | “Something genuinely time-bound is happening.” | During a real campaign, this creates a timely reason to act after visitors have seen products. Outside that window it should disappear automatically. |
| Shop editorial / visit-us story | “There are knowledgeable people and a real place behind this.” | This is the fuller trust proof and a visual pause after commerce. DTC research supports it, while placing it here avoids delaying the first shopping paths. |
| Shop by Intention | “I can browse by the need or feeling that brought me here.” | This is the simpler fallback for visitors who did not use the Oracle. Keeping the two intention-led routes apart prevents the page asking almost the same question twice in succession. |
| Written in the Stars | “I want the zodiac route.” | Twelve sign choices make this the longest and narrowest discovery lens. It remains available without defining the shop or displacing broader routes. |

The transitions follow a simple progression: identity → practical confidence → catalogue map → guided help → products → timely campaign → human proof → secondary discovery routes.

## Live journey observed

The live homepage was inspected on 14 July 2026 at 1440 × 900 and 390 × 844. Section-heading positions were measured from the top of the page.

| Section | Desktop y | Mobile y | Observation |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Hero heading | 294 | 286 | Strong identity and two routes, but the mobile opening delays the collection heading until about 1.35 viewports down. |
| Browse the Collections | 988 | 1,150 | Clear two-column mobile wayfinder and the best broad map of the small catalogue. |
| New Manifestations | 2,344 | 2,054 | About two mobile screens for eight products; the content does not currently support the promise of “new”. |
| Shop by Intention | 3,782 | 3,735 | Strong branded 2 × 3 choice grid, but another long discovery surface. |
| Shop story | 4,559 | 5,155 | Persuasive and specific, roughly one mobile screen. |
| Oracle | 5,395 | 5,942 | Distinctive and ready for interaction, but reached after roughly seven mobile screens unless the hero anchor is used. |

The sticky mobile header keeps navigation available, but it does not solve the cost of reaching a buried homepage feature. Moving the Oracle forward and cutting New Manifestations to four products on mobile would recover much more useful height than reordering full-length sections unchanged.

## Credible alternative: returning-customer first

1. Hero
2. Trust bar
3. New Manifestations
4. Browse the Collections
5. Consult the Oracle
6. Ritual countdown, conditional
7. Shop editorial / visit-us story
8. Shop by Intention
9. Written in the Stars

This gives regular customers the quickest route to recent stock and may suit a business with frequent small drops and a high share of returning homepage visitors. The cost is weaker orientation for first-time visitors: usability testing shows that a narrow promotion or “new arrivals” path can cause mobile users to underestimate the range or mistake a scoped list for the whole catalogue. Choose it only if analytics show returning visitors dominate and the new-stock range is kept genuinely current. Until then, the live `all` collection and its July product mix do not justify this order.

## Evidence

### 1. The homepage must reveal the catalogue before narrow promotions define it

[Baymard's Homepage & Category Navigation study](https://baymard.com/research/homepage-and-category-usability) reports 25 rounds of qualitative think-aloud testing and more than 4,400 participant/site sessions. Its current research catalogue benchmarks 334 leading ecommerce sites. Product-finding failures repeatedly caused abandonment. The study treats the homepage and category structure as core product-finding surfaces.

The most current public benchmark, [updated 30 September 2025](https://baymard.com/blog/ecommerce-navigation-best-practice), contains more than 16,000 manually scored homepage and category-navigation elements and more than 13,000 examples across 180+ leading US and European sites. It finds mediocre-to-poor performance on 58% of desktop sites and 67% of mobile sites. In mobile testing, featured links with an unclear scope caused people to mistake a narrow list for the whole range and sometimes abandon.

A narrower [homepage product-breadth analysis, updated 10 April 2025](https://baymard.com/blog/inferring-product-catalog-from-homepage), adds a survey of 1,167 US online shoppers. Thirty-three per cent said they would begin casual browsing for a visually driven product by scrolling the homepage. Baymard's tests found that users could infer range from either representative categories or products, but that the homepage should show roughly 40–50% of product types. It gives a direct counterexample in which sale and new-product content displaced catalogue breadth and participants struggled to understand the range.

Inference for Totally Spellbound: keep Browse the Collections above New Manifestations. The collection tiles supply the broad map; the product row can then create immediacy without accidentally presenting “new” as “all”. This evidence supports relative priority, not an exact pixel position or a guaranteed conversion lift.

Limits: Baymard's qualitative work identifies recurring usability failure modes rather than sales causality. Its benchmarks skew towards large retailers. The 2025 survey is US-based, while Totally Spellbound is a small UK shop with a specialist audience.

### 2. Mobile visitors scan for an overview, but every section still consumes scarce attention

In an earlier mobile study, [Baymard observed 70% of test participants scroll nearly the whole homepage to get an overview](https://baymard.com/blog/mobile-homepage-usability) (published 17 February 2016). Its newer 2025 work explains the continuing mechanism: the small viewport creates “tunnel vision”, and mobile users can lose track of whether a link represents the whole range or a narrow scope. In the [2024 product-finding research refresh](https://baymard.com/blog/product-finding-2024-launch), Baymard spent 5,550+ research hours and ran 219 qualitative participant/site sessions across 12 sites to revalidate and update its findings.

Inference for Totally Spellbound: mobile users may reach lower sections, but that does not make the order harmless. Put the broad, low-commitment routes first. Shorten the opening hero enough to reveal that shopping continues below, then let each successive section ask for slightly more interpretation or interaction.

Limits: the 70% figure is old and does not report ecommerce conversion. The 2024 refresh gives the aggregate method, not a section-order experiment.

### 3. Practical trust belongs early; brand story should support shopping rather than delay it

Baymard's [DTC benchmark](https://baymard.com/blog/dtc-benchmark), published 22 February 2022, manually assessed 36 sites against 485 research-based parameters, producing 17,000+ DTC usability scores. Its tests found that visitors expected the homepage to preview products, distinctive brand attributes and important policies. Participants noticed shipping, returns and warranty information, and Baymard reports that missing key product or brand information led to lost interest and abandonment. The same study says visitors to niche brands value understanding the brand more deeply, while still describing the homepage, navigation and product lists as core product-finding surfaces.

Baymard's [shipping and returns research, updated with 2025 data](https://baymard.com/blog/footer-needs-return-shipping-links), draws on more than 1,000 moderated usability sessions. It found that shipping cost, delivery timing and returns can be central to purchase confidence, and that these answers should be available through several routes. Its current [2026 product-page synthesis](https://baymard.com/blog/current-state-ecommerce-product-page-ux) reports a separate quantitative study of 1,032 respondents in which 60% looked for returns information on the product page.

Inference for Totally Spellbound: a concise trust bar directly after the hero can answer practical doubts before visitors choose a path. The fuller shop/visit story is better after the first commerce modules: it proves the human, physical business without making a ready-to-shop visitor work through an origin story first.

Limits: the DTC set mixes small niche brands with global manufacturers and is only partly analogous to an independent multi-category retailer. The policy studies establish that people seek the information, but do not establish that a homepage trust bar in position two produces more sales. Claims in the bar must be specific and true.

### 4. “Choice overload” does not justify hiding the range; use structure and optional guidance

Scheibehenne, Greifeneder and Todd's peer-reviewed [2010 meta-analysis of choice overload](https://scheibehenne.com/ScheibehenneGreifenederTodd2010.pdf) covered 50 published and unpublished experiments, 63 conditions and 5,036 participants. The overall effect was virtually zero (`d = 0.02`, 95% CI `-0.09` to `0.12`) with substantial variation between studies. The authors could not identify sufficient general conditions under which more options reliably reduced choice or satisfaction.

Baymard's 2025 navigation testing offers the more practical distinction: participants became overwhelmed by long, unstructured category lists, often at around ten or more subcategory options, while representative categories and thumbnails improved overview and scanning.

Inference for Totally Spellbound: the answer is a clear sequence of different shopping lenses, not simply fewer products. Collections gives a stable catalogue map. New Manifestations supplies a small concrete edit. Shop by Intention and the Oracle offer optional decision support for visitors whose preferences are less formed.

Limits: the meta-analysis is old, spans many non-commerce tasks and does not test homepage modules. It mainly warns against treating “fewer choices convert better” as a universal law.

### 5. The top of a long page receives disproportionate attention

Nielsen Norman Group's [2018 eye-tracking analysis](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/scrolling-and-attention/) covered more than 130,000 fixations from 120 participants across ecommerce, news, blogs, FAQs and reference pages. Participants spent 57% of page-viewing time above the fold and 74% within the first two screenfuls. NN/g explicitly recommends reserving the top for high-priority user and business goals while testing page length with representative users.

Contentsquare's [2025 Digital Experience Benchmark](https://contentsquare.com/press/2025-digital-experience-benchmarks/), published 28 January 2025, analysed 90 billion sessions, 389 billion page views and 6,000 websites worldwide, comparing Q4 2023 with Q4 2024. Sites whose session depth rose by at least 10% saw an average 5.4% conversion increase. This is correlation, but it reinforces the commercial value of helping people reach useful next pages rather than making homepage depth the goal.

Inference for Totally Spellbound: put the broad collection route and the distinctive guided route in the high-attention part of the journey. Shorter sections matter as much as their order. The business outcome is a useful product or collection visit, not reaching the footer.

Limits: NN/g's study is eight years old, used a desktop eye tracker and spans many page types. Contentsquare's aggregate cannot establish that deeper sessions cause conversion or which homepage component created the depth.

### 6. A quiz or oracle is a brand asset and a hypothesis, not a proven universal conversion module

No current, independent large-sample study was found that establishes a universal sales lift or optimal homepage position for an ecommerce quiz. Vendor case studies tend to report selected clients and cannot separate the tool from offer, traffic and merchandising differences.

The defensible evidence is indirect: mobile users need clear broad paths; structured guidance can help uncertain users; and specialist-brand visitors value deeper brand and product understanding. None of that proves that every visitor should enter an interactive flow.

Inference for Totally Spellbound: move Consult the Oracle directly after Browse the Collections. The ordinary catalogue map comes first; the Oracle then offers help before a long product grid. It stays optional, and the hero's secondary link still provides a direct jump. Measure starts, completions, result clicks and purchases rather than treating interaction as success. This is the least evidence-backed part of the proposed order and should be reversed if its outcomes are empty, irrelevant or weak.

### 7. A countdown must be truthful, relevant and conditional

The UK's [CMA guidance on unfair commercial practices](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/unfair-commercial-practices-cma207/unfair-commercial-practices), updated 18 November 2025, explains the consumer rules in force since 6 April 2025 under the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024. It lists as a banned practice falsely saying that an offer is available for a limited time to force an immediate decision. A resetting countdown is the guidance's explicit example.

This is active enforcement, not a theoretical concern. On 22 May 2026, the CMA secured a [High Court-confirmed settlement with Emma Sleep](https://www.gov.uk/cma-cases/emma-group-consumer-protection-case) after the company admitted using misleading countdown timers, “high demand” messages and discount claims that created false pressure.

The CMA's broader [2022 online choice architecture evidence review](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/online-choice-architecture-how-digital-design-can-harm-competition-and-consumers/evidence-review-of-online-choice-architecture-and-consumer-and-competition-harm) rates evidence on scarcity and popularity claims as strong and notes that time pressure can make people pursue urgency over importance.

Inference for Totally Spellbound: do not give Ritual countdown a permanent place merely because urgency can prompt action. While the Lammas event is current and linked to relevant stock, use it as a campaign slot after New Manifestations. At zero, or whenever there is no maintained campaign, hide the section. A truthful calendar countdown may create atmosphere; it still needs to earn its place against clearer shopping paths.

Limits: the CMA material concerns consumer harm and legal compliance, not the conversion performance of a truthful ritual countdown. This is research, not legal advice.

## Trade-offs

- Collections before new stock favours first-time understanding and mobile orientation. New stock first favours regulars who already know the range.
- Story after shopping routes respects purchase intent. Moving the real-shop story higher could build trust for cold traffic, but it also delays products and needs evidence from this site's analytics before doing so.
- Oracle higher makes the homepage distinctive and helps uncertain shoppers sooner. It risks spending prime space on an interaction whose sales effect is unproven; keeping Collections above it protects ordinary browsing.
- Intention and zodiac late reduce repetition in the opening journey. They give those routes less exposure, which is acceptable until click and revenue data show they outperform broader paths.
- Countdown conditional protects trust and keeps the page evergreen. It gives up a standing urgency cue, which is appropriate unless a real deadline is present and maintained.

## Move, shorten, combine or defer

| Section | Treatment before or during a reorder |
| --- | --- |
| Hero | Keep first. Shorten mobile height enough to show the next section is coming. Keep one clear shopping action and the Oracle jump. |
| Trust bar | Keep second, but compress its mobile height. The current claims concern packing, payment, family ownership and help; do not imply that it already answers delivery or returns. The fuller human proof remains in the story section. |
| Browse the Collections | Keep all six categories and move nothing above it except identity and concise trust. |
| Consult the Oracle | Move from seventh to fourth, after confirming all intention/element combinations lead to useful in-stock results. |
| New Manifestations | Shorten from eight products to four on mobile and no more than six on desktop. Curate by genuine recency and availability; the live `all` source does not currently do that. |
| Ritual countdown | Move into the campaign slot after products while Lammas is current. Hide automatically at zero and leave the slot absent when Alison has no maintained campaign. |
| Shop story | Keep as a separate proof section, but shorten the homepage copy and link to Visit Us for detail. It should feel like a pause, not another full landing page. |
| Shop by Intention | Move below the story so it does not repeat the Oracle's first question immediately. Keep the six choices. |
| Written in the Stars | Keep last. Shorten the full 12-sign treatment or test a compact teaser linking to the zodiac route; defer a redesign until collection depth and click data justify it. |

## First implementation questions

1. What share of homepage sessions and revenue comes from new versus returning visitors, split by mobile and desktop?
2. Which current homepage sections produce product views, add-to-cart events and purchases, rather than only clicks or dwell time?
3. Should New Manifestations be a manually curated collection, an automated newest-first collection, or renamed to match what `all` actually returns? Who removes sold-out and off-season items?
4. What exact delivery, collection, dispatch and returns claims can the trust bar make without qualification?
5. Do all Oracle intention/element combinations return enough relevant, in-stock products, and can start, completion, result click and downstream purchase be measured?
6. How many distinct, worthwhile products sit behind each intention and zodiac collection? Would a compact zodiac teaser be more honest than 12 equal homepage choices?
7. Who owns the seasonal slot, what happens automatically at zero, and can “no active campaign” be the safe default within Alison's 30-minute weekly admin limit?
8. Can the reorder be evaluated for at least one complete trading cycle against a preserved baseline, without adding a new app or maintenance workflow?

## What this research does not establish

No high-trust source found a universally best ecommerce homepage order, and no source tested Totally Spellbound's exact components or audience. The opening recommendation has direct usability support. The lower-half sequence is a reasoned hypothesis based on commitment, trust and brand fit. Before a permanent change, preserve a baseline and judge the reorder against product discovery, add-to-cart rate, completed orders and revenue per homepage session, with mobile and new visitors reported separately.
